CHAPTER THREE: DON'T FEEL THEIR PAIN, LABEL IT

In this chapter, Chris Voss discusses the importance of identifying and labeling emotions in negotiation. Here are some key lessons from this chapter:

Here are some key lessons from this chapter summarized by ChatGPT:

  1. Labeling emotions helps diffuse tension: By acknowledging and identifying the other person's emotions, you can help diffuse tension and create a more productive negotiating environment.
  2. Use "I" statements when labeling emotions: Using "I" statements, such as "I feel frustrated when...", can help avoid confrontational language and create a sense of understanding and empathy.
  3. Be specific when labeling emotions: Using specific emotion labels, such as "frustrated" or "anxious", can be more effective than general terms like "upset" or "angry".
  4. Use labeling to gather information: Asking open-ended questions and labeling the other person's emotions can help you gather more information and better understand their perspective.
  5. Be careful not to overuse emotion labeling: While labeling emotions can be a useful technique, it's important not to overuse it or it can come across as insincere or manipulative.

How to Create Trust with Tactical Empathy:

  • Image yourself in your counterpart's situation. The beauty of empathy is that it doesn't demand that you agree with the other person's idea (you may well find them crazy). But by acknowledging the other person's situation, you immediately convey that you are listening. And once they know that you are listening, they may tell you something that you can use.
  • The reasons why a counterpart will not make an agreement with you are often more powerful than why they willmake a deal, so focus first on clearing the barriers to agreement. Denying barriers or negative influences gives them credence; get them into the open.
  • Pause. After you label a barrier or mirror a statement, let it sink in. Don't worry, the other party will fill the silence.
  • Label your counterpart's fears to difusse their power. We all want to talk about happy stuf, but remember, the faster you interrupt action in your counterpart's amygdala, the part of the brain that generates fear, the faster you can generate feelings of safety, well-being, and trust.
  • List the worst things that the other party could say about you and say them before the other person can. Performing an accusation audit in advance prepares you to head off negative dynamics before they take root. And becuase these accusations often sound exaggerated when said aloud, speaking them will encourage the other person to claim that quite the opposite is true.
  • Remember you're dealing with a person who wants to be appreciated and understood. So use labels to reinforce and encourage positive perceptions and dynamics.
